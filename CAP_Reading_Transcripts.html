Applied technique

Which agent produced this output, and which instructions can I change?

Reading transcripts as a CAP builder is different from reading them as a QA reviewer. The question changes: not “what went wrong on this call?” but “which part of the system produced this behavior, and what would I change?” This doc teaches you the technique — what the two artifacts are, what to look at first, and the patterns you’ll see often enough to recognize on sight.

Reading transcripts is something you already know how to do from QA. Reading them as a CAP builder is different. The CAP builder is asking, every step of the way, which agent is producing this output, what instructions is that agent following, and which of those instructions can I change.
The two artifacts

Every call leaves two diagnostic records.

You need both. Each one alone is incomplete. Diagnosing only from the transcript is the most common technique mistake new hires make.

T

Transcript

The text record of what was spoken.

Tells you: what the caller said, what the AIR said, and in what order.
Does NOT tell you: which agent was active at any given moment.
E

Event log

The per-call record of every agent invocation, function call, parameter, and response.

Tells you: which agent ran when, which functions fired, and what was returned.
Does NOT show: the spoken content.
Reading them in tandem

Both windows open. Always.

Use the event log to identify which agent was active at each moment. Use the transcript to see what the agent was doing during that period. When you spot a divergence in the transcript, jump to the event log to see what was happening in the agent at that moment.

The methodology

A reliable approach for any problematic call.

1
Read the transcript end to end first

Do not stop at the first weird thing. Read it the way the caller would have experienced it.

2
Articulate the goal

One sentence. Same reflex from the Diagnostic Mindset doc.

3
Open the event log

Identify the agent transitions. Where did Main hand to Intake? Did the FAQ Agent get invoked? Was there an Escalation? A Lookup?

4
Map the divergence to the active agent

When the call went sideways, which agent was running? That agent's instructions are where the cause lives.

5
Pull up the relevant instructions

If the active agent receives the CAP, look at the CAP. Look at the playbook. Look at the dashboard config. One of those produced the behavior.

6
Hypothesize, test, confirm

Form a hypothesis about what change would produce different behavior. Test it. Confirm.

Steps 4 and 5 are where new hires can shave hours off their diagnoses with practice.
The rule that explains half the complaints

Field collection.

The single most common source of "the AIR ignored what I said" complaints is the field collection rule. Memorize it.

A field is treated as collected only when ALL of these are true. If any one is missing, the field is NOT collected.

COLLECTED when

  • The Intake Assistant explicitly asked for it
  • The caller answered in response to that question
  • The exchange happened during intake in the current call

NOT collected when

  • The caller mentioned it in their opening utterance
  • The caller mentioned it inside an FAQ exchange
  • The caller mentioned it during a transfer request
  • It came from EXTRA CALL CONTEXT (pre-populated profile data)
  • It came from caller ID
  • It is implied by call metadata
This is by design. When a transcript shows the AIR asking for something the caller "already said," this rule is almost always why. Your first move when you see that pattern is to confirm the rule applies, not to assume the AIR is broken.
Patterns to recognize

Five patterns you'll see often.

When you see a known pattern, your diagnosis time drops from minutes to seconds. Each one almost always classifies as working-as-designed, except where noted.

01

"They already told us their name"

What you see Caller opens with "Hi, this is John, I had a fender bender." Intake Assistant later asks for the full name.
Why Opening utterances do not count as collected fields.
Verdict Working as designed
Fix path If the account wants it changed: a CAP that explicitly extracts opening-utterance content and treats it as collected.
02

"Pre-populated data ignored"

What you see A returning caller has email pre-populated in EXTRA CALL CONTEXT. Intake Assistant still asks for the email.
Why EXTRA CALL CONTEXT is background data for verification, not collected data.
Verdict Working as designed
Fix path Usually none. CAPs that try to skip fields based on EXTRA CALL CONTEXT fight the native rule and produce unreliable behavior.
03

"Qualified, then started over"

What you see A Service-based qualification step ran, the caller qualified, the matched playbook loaded. The Intake Assistant then re-asks fields the qualification flow already touched.
Why When a new playbook loads, step state resets. The new playbook may have STEP 1 fields different from anything collected during qualification.
Verdict Working as designed
Fix path Usually none. The reset is required for playbook-step integrity.
04

"Re-asked after a transfer attempt"

What you see Caller went into intake, gave name and phone, then requested a transfer. The Escalation Agent ran, returned (transfer didn't fire). Intake Assistant resumes and asks for name and phone again.
Why This is the one case where re-asking is NOT correct system behavior. If name and phone were collected during intake, they are collected and should not be re-asked. The transfer delegation rules say so explicitly.
Verdict Likely a CAP issue
Fix path Look at any CAP rules around the transfer attempt. The CAP may be overriding the resumption behavior. Could also be a bug.
05

"Main decided X, Intake did Y"

What you see Main Assistant classifies the caller as new client, routes to the New Client playbook. Mid-intake, the caller reveals they are existing. The system reloads the playbook as Existing Client. Step state resets.
Why When caller type changes mid-call, the playbook reloads. There is no shared state between Main's original classification and Intake's behavior on the new playbook.
Verdict Working as designed
Fix path Usually none. The reload is correct.
Worked example

The full methodology, applied.

Scenario. HVAC client. CS forwarded a complaint that the AIR isn't handling emergency calls correctly.

AIR: "Thanks for calling [Client Name]. How can I help?"

Caller: "My pipe burst, I need someone out here now."

AIR: "I'd be happy to help. What's your full name?"

Caller: "Wait, this is an emergency, can you just send someone out?"

AIR: "I just need to collect a few details first. What's your full name?"

[call continues with standard intake]

Apply the methodology
  1. Read end to end. Done above.
  2. Goal. Caller wants emergency service dispatched immediately.
  3. Event log. Main Assistant ran, did not detect insistence (the caller offered information about the burst pipe alongside the request, which doesn't qualify as insistence). Handed to Intake. Intake is running with the standard playbook.
  4. Divergence. Turn 3, where the AIR asked for the name instead of routing to emergency handling.
  5. Active agent and instructions. Intake Assistant. Now we go to the CAP.
This is where the diagnostic move matters. There are two possible causes, with different fixes. The diagnostic move that distinguishes them: open the CAP. Read the rules at the top.
Possibility A

The CAP has no emergency routing.

Looking at the CAP, there's no rule that detects emergency keywords or routes them to a different flow. The Intake Assistant is doing exactly what it's supposed to: running the standard intake playbook.

Classification: CAP issue (missing logic).
Fix: Add a CAP rule that detects emergency phrasing in the opening utterance and routes to an emergency flow.
Possibility B

The CAP has it, but it didn't fire.

The trigger conditions in the CAP are too narrow. Maybe the CAP says "if caller mentions emergency" but "burst pipe" isn't in the trigger list.

Classification: CAP issue (incomplete trigger conditions).
Fix: Expand the trigger list to cover common emergency phrasings ("burst pipe," "leak," "flooding," "no heat," "no water," etc.).
The transcript-reading habit this teaches

Don't stop at "the CAP didn't handle this." Ask whether the CAP didn't try, or tried and missed. Different fixes.

Anti-patterns

What to stop doing.

Quote-mining

Treating a single line as the full story. A transcript is a narrative; one line out of context is misleading.

Skipping the event log

Diagnosing only from the transcript. You'll guess about active agents and you'll be wrong.

Reading without knowing which agent was active

Same as above. Worth saying twice.

Rushing to the divergence

Reading the first few turns then jumping to "the bad part." You'll miss the precondition that produced it.

Ignoring caller affect

"Caller (audibly annoyed)" or "Caller (interrupting)" are diagnostic signals. They tell you when the caller's expectation diverged from the AIR's behavior.

Stopping at "the CAP didn't handle this"

Always ask: didn't try, or tried and missed?

Building the muscle

Three habits worth training.

Read with the event log open

Get used to having both visible. Resist the urge to "just check the transcript real quick."

Pattern-match before you reason

When you see a transcript, ask first: have I seen this pattern? If yes, your diagnosis drops to seconds. If no, run the full methodology.

Time yourself, but not too early

While you're building fluency, take all the time you need. Once fluent, aim for 3 to 5 minutes per call for routine diagnoses. Anything taking longer than 15 minutes probably means you're missing something or misclassifying.
