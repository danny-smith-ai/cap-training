Conceptual foundation

The customization layer for AIR.

A CAP (Custom AIR Prompt) is text inserted into a specific configuration field. At runtime, that text gets injected into three specific agents and changes how they behave. This is how Smith.ai delivers client-specific AI behavior beyond what the dashboard can configure.

Definition

What is a CAP?

Two ways to say the same thing. Use the plain version with colleagues. Work with the technical version daily.

Plain language

Custom instructions for the AIR.

A CAP is a set of custom instructions that change how the AI Receptionist behaves for a specific client. The AIR is built as an out-of-the-box product with a specific way of handling calls. Some clients need behavior outside that default. CAPs are how we get that custom behavior without breaking the underlying product.

Technical

Text in SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS.

A CAP is text inserted into a specific configuration field called SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS on a client account. At runtime, the Smith.ai system injects this text into the system prompts of three specific agents. Those agents then behave according to the CAP's instructions in addition to their baseline programming.

Why CAPs exist

The bridge between default and specific.

The AIR has a primary prompt that handles most use cases well out of the box. But Smith.ai serves clients across legal, medical, home services, and many other industries, each with their own intake logic, transfer rules, and language conventions. CAPs exist for three reasons.

01

Specific needs the default can't meet.

A personal injury law firm doesn't qualify callers the same way an HVAC company does. Default behavior is generic by design. CAPs make it specific.

02

Beyond the dashboard.

The dashboard handles a lot of configuration, but conditional logic, custom phrasing, multi-practice flows, and many transfer rules need prompt-level instructions instead.

03

Competitive advantage.

No other AI receptionist on the market offers this depth of customization. CAPs are a meaningful part of why clients choose Smith.ai over alternatives.

What this means

CAPs cannot directly modify what's on the right.

If a request asks to change how appointments are scheduled mid-call, change the transfer handoff message, or modify the dashboard FAQ behavior, a CAP cannot do those things directly.

What CAPs can do is shape behavior in the agents that DO receive SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS, including the moments BEFORE they hand off to a restricted agent and AFTER they get control back.

What a CAP can do

Inside the three reachable agents:

  • Tell the Main Assistant whether or not to invoke the FAQ Agent (startFAQSession)
  • Tell the Main Assistant or Intake Assistant when to invoke the Scheduling Agent (startSchedulingSession)
  • Tell the Intake Assistant when to invoke the Escalation Agent for a transfer
Worked example

How "FAQ Customization" actually works.

The native FAQ system in the dashboard can hold up to 50 FAQs. When an account needs more, additional Q&A pairs go directly into the CAP. The Main Assistant or Intake Assistant (both of which receive SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS) then answer those CAP-housed FAQs themselves, without involving the dashboard FAQ system. Customization happens in the agents that receive the CAP, not in the ones that don't.

What CAPs can do

The six capability categories.

Every CAP request falls into one or more of these. Memorize the categories. They are the shared vocabulary you'll use with CS and COB.

CATEGORY 01

Call Transfer Handling

Conditional and rule-based transfers. Deciding when to transfer a human, when not to, and to whom.

  • Conditional and rule-based transfers
  • Prevent unnecessary human transfers
  • Schedule vs transfer logic
  • Transfer to person or department if mentioned
Example requests
  • I want callers with billing issues transferred to a certain number
  • If they mention Sales Department, transfer the caller
CATEGORY 02

Custom Call Flows

Different paths through the call based on caller type, practice area, or other branching logic.

  • Conditional call flows
  • Custom caller types
  • Practice area-based flows
  • Renaming caller types
Example requests
  • If they're calling for an emergency, follow these instructions instead
  • I need specific intake questions depending on my 4 practice areas
CATEGORY 03

Custom End of Call Messaging

What the AIR says before ending the call, especially when the message should differ based on what happened during the call.

  • Conditionally delivered closing remarks
  • Custom end messages per scenario
Example requests
  • If a PNC is calling for parts, give this final message instead
  • If the caller is disqualified, I want the AI to say this…
CATEGORY 04

FAQ Customization

Modifying how FAQ-style questions get answered. Most common form: housing additional Q&A pairs in the CAP when an account needs more than the 50 the dashboard can hold natively.

  • Provide answers longer than 500 characters
  • Avoid transferring too early without answering FAQs
  • Ensure verbatim answers
  • Trigger FAQs based on keywords
Example requests
  • The AI is editorializing my FAQ answers
  • The bot keeps transferring after answering a question
CATEGORY 05

Script Customization

Custom phrasing for things the AIR says. The "voice" of the AIR for a specific client.

  • Custom phrasing for questions
  • Intake structure by practice area
Example requests
  • When gathering the email, I want you to say this…
  • I need the bot to use bridging phrases between questions
CATEGORY 06

Confirmation Customization

Whether and how the AIR confirms what it heard back to the caller.

  • Enable/disable confirmation
  • Enable/disable reflective listening
Example requests
  • I don't want the AI to confirm spelling back to the caller
  • I need the bot to always confirm the spelling of the business name
Limits

What CAPs cannot do (yet).

These are common requests CAPs currently cannot fulfill. When a request lands in this category, log it as a feature request (Canny) and let the requester know the workaround status.

Out of reach

Four current limitations.

  • Custom call transfer handoff message. What the AIR says when transferring is generated by the Escalation Agent, which doesn't receive SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS.
  • Conditional SMS follow-up. SMS routing is a platform feature, not a prompt feature.
  • Conditional call summaries. Summary routing (e.g., PNCs to one email, ECs to another) is a platform feature.
  • Conditional actions based on date/time. CAPs don't have access to runtime date/time information in a way that lets them branch on it.
Example requests that fall here
  • When transferring a call, I want it to say: Great, that's all I need…
  • I only want to send the follow-up SMS if they don't want to schedule
  • I want PNC summaries sent to this email and EC to a different email
  • I only want transfers to me on Tuesdays and Thursdays within 9am-2pm
Where requests come from

The intake path.

Custom Prompt Request form.

CAP requests come into the team primarily through the Custom Prompt Request form, submitted by CS or COB on behalf of a client. The full intake-to-deploy workflow is covered in the CAP Modification Playbook.

  • Most requests originate from CS or COB after a client conversation
  • Requests should be specific. A pasted client comment or a forwarded email is not a request, it's raw material.
  • Some requests have prerequisites (e.g., transfer-related requests usually require team directory updates first)
  • Bug reports and feature requests adjacent to the workaround should be linked from Canny
Common misunderstandings

What gets confused.

These come up frequently from CS and COB. Knowing how to handle them is part of the job.

01

"Can't a CAP just do X?"

When X requires reaching into a restricted agent. The architectural truth is the answer. Translate the request: is it really about that agent, or about the agent that hands off to it?

02

CAP changes vs dashboard changes.

Some things belong in the Smith.ai dashboard, not in a CAP. Renaming caller types, enabling certain transfer behaviors, configuring the team directory are dashboard tasks. Putting them in a CAP is an anti-pattern.

03

"We just want it to be smarter."

Vague capability requests are not actionable. The job is to translate "smarter" into a specific behavior change in a specific category.

04

CAPs as general AI tweaks.

A CAP is not "telling the AI to do better." It's prompt engineering against a known multi-agent architecture with known constraints. Imprecision in the CAP becomes imprecision in the call.

05

Conditional logic on missing runtime data.

Date, time, caller history beyond classification, and external state are generally not available for CAP branching.
